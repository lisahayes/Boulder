Yard Leak Detection & Repair in Boulder, CO

Summer is when Boulder yards confess. The lawn browns on schedule under the watering rules, except for one stripe that stays defiantly green, or one corner that squishes in a week without rain. The yard is telling you a buried line is watering it off the books.

The Lawn as a Diagnostic Instrument

Turf reads soil moisture better than any meter, and in a climate where summer lawns run thirsty, the contrast is diagnostic gold. A stripe of lush growth tracing a straight line marks a leaking run along its length. A circle of vigor marks a point failure. Moss, mushrooms, or algae in a sunny spot mark chronic saturation. Squish underfoot in dry weather marks the water table a leak has built locally. Even winter reads: a strip where snow melts first is warm or wet ground announcing itself. We ask for lawn photos before the visit for exactly this reason, and the best ones solve half the case.

What Runs Under a Boulder Yard

Yard leaks come from a specific cast of lines. Hose bib feeds crossing to remote spigots and detached garages. The dedicated run to a yard hydrant. Lines serving sheds, studios, and the accessory units Boulder lots increasingly carry. Old abandoned runs, to a removed pool, a former garden setup, that were capped badly or not at all. The irrigation system's pressurized main between the backflow and the valves. And the big one, the water service itself, which has its own page and its own stakes. Sorting which cast member is leaking starts with valve-by-valve isolation at the meter and the manifolds, watching which shutoff makes the loss stop.

Timing Games: The Watering-Schedule Tell

Boulder's summer watering rules create a rhythm we use diagnostically. Lawns get watered before 10 a.m. or after 6 p.m., so soil moisture from legitimate irrigation follows that clock and dries through the day. A leak keeps no schedule. Wetness that persists at 3 p.m. in a zone that ran at dawn, or a meter that creeps at midday with the controller idle, separates the leak from the watering it hides behind. Owners can run this observation themselves in one afternoon, and it is the cheapest instrument on this page.

Locating in Lawn, Bed, and Under Hardscape

Once isolation names the guilty line, locating marks it. The run gets traced so the search follows pipe instead of hunches, then acoustic ground listening works the line and peaks over the escape point. Leaks under walkways and driveways get the tracer-gas treatment through the concrete. Expansive clay complicates the surface evidence, absorbing early leakage and surfacing it downslope, so the instruments outrank the wet spot every time, the same lesson our underground page teaches at length. The flag goes in the grass within a foot or two, and the shovel work shrinks to match.

Repairs That Respect the Landscape

A located yard repair is one neat excavation: sod lifted and saved, the bad section removed and new pipe fit for burial set in its place, bedding packed right, sod returned. Runs that have failed repeatedly, or old lines in materials past their era, get replacement quotes alongside the patch. Abandoned lines get properly capped at their source so they stop haunting future summers. And every repair notes what the line serves, because a leak on the run to a spigot nobody uses is a chance to retire pipe rather than fix it. Where the loss traces to the irrigation system or the main service line instead, the finding hands off to those scopes with the isolation work already done. Yard Line Questions From Boulder Lawns

How can I tell if the wet spot is my yard line or my neighbor's?

Isolation answers it. Shut your main at the meter: if the wetness keeps being fed, the water is not yours, and the conversation moves next door with evidence instead of accusation. Meter tests on both properties settle it definitively, and we handle those diplomatically often.

The yard hydrant drips constantly at the spout. Is that a buried leak?

Different problem, same urgency. A weeping hydrant usually means its plunger seal is worn, but a hydrant that will not stop can also be draining its line into the soil at the base, which is buried leakage. Both are repairable at the head or the standpipe without replacing the run.

Can I just cap off the leaking line instead of repairing it?

If the line serves nothing you use, absolutely, and it is the cheapest fix on the menu. The cap must happen at the source connection, not at the leak, so the dead run holds no pressure. We verify what the line feeds before capping so nobody discovers the answer next July. Sort yours out at (303) 552-3896.
