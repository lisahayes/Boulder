Acoustic Leak Detection in Boulder, CO

A leak is a sound source before it is a wet spot. Water forced through a breach under pressure vibrates the pipe wall, and that vibration travels the pipe and the ground around it as sound. Acoustic detection is the craft of following that sound home.

The Physics, Briefly

Every pressurized leak produces noise in two bands. The breach itself hisses as water jets through, a higher-frequency signal that travels well along the pipe. And the escaping water churns against soil or bedding, a lower rumble that radiates up through the ground. Pipe material and pressure shape both: metal pipe carries the pipe-borne signal for impressive distances, plastic dampens it and forces more reliance on the ground-borne band, and higher pressure means louder everything. Boulder's gravity-fed system helps here, since lots with generous static pressure make their leaks audible in a way low-pressure systems never do.

The Listening Kit

The tools amplify what ears alone cannot reach. Listening discs and probes contact valves, hydrants, and exposed pipe, reading the pipe-borne signal at each access point to bracket the leak between the loudest two. Ground microphones then work the surface along the traced line, and the operator reads signal strength step by step until it peaks over the breach. Filters strip wind, traffic, and footsteps from the signal, and headphones plus a display let the operator confirm by ear and by meter together. The craft is real: knowing what a leak sounds like versus a partly closed valve, flow noise, or an electrical hum is experience, not equipment.

What Acoustic Finds Best

The method shines on pressurized lines under soil and concrete: service lines, irrigation mains, pool loops, and the under-slab supply runs that produce warm floors. It performs best when the line's path is known, which is why locating and tracing come first. It also wants a quiet, pressurized system, so we work with fixtures off and, on stubborn cases, at night when the neighborhood's noise floor drops. The classic assignment, the slab leak heard through the kitchen floor, and the buried service-line break flagged in a lawn, are both acoustic work at heart.

Where Sound Needs Help

Honesty about limits keeps diagnoses fast. Very small leaks in plastic pipe can run nearly silent. Deep lines bury their signal. Fixture noise, appliance hum, and wind fight the operator. And gravity drains, carrying no pressure, make no usable sound at all, which routes those problems to dye and camera work instead. When the acoustic survey narrows a leak but cannot close, the case escalates to tracer gas or thermal confirmation rather than to guessing, and the combination reaches the mark that any single tool might miss. That escalation logic is the heart of a proper detection visit, not a failure of it.

What You Get

An acoustic survey ends in specifics: a marked point on floor or ground, a confidence read, photos, and a repair plan sized to the mark. If your leak has a sound, we will find where it is singing from.

Meter reading, by the way, is a listening skill of its own. The small triangle or star dial on most registers spins at flows the numbered dials cannot show, and learning to read yours is a free acoustic instrument every household already owns.

Depth of experience matters more in this method than any other on the truck, because the gear amplifies everything and the operator decides what counts. Ours have logged the hours, and the marks carry that history every time one goes down.

Timing helps the ears as much as gear does. Early morning surveys beat afternoons, winter beats summer for indoor slab work with the furnace cycling predictably, and a house emptied of running appliances for one hour gives the operator the floor. We will ask for that hour, and it pays.

You can even pre-screen at home: press a long screwdriver's handle to your ear and its tip to the meter or a valve at night. Steady hiss with everything off is worth a call, and you will have heard your leak before we do.

Acoustic Method Questions From Boulder

Can acoustic detection find leaks in plastic pipe?

Yes, with adjusted expectations. Plastic dampens pipe-borne sound, so the survey leans on ground microphones and on maximizing pressure and quiet. Small plastic-line leaks are the method's hardest case, and those escalate to tracer gas, which does not care what the pipe is made of.

How accurate is the mark you make?

On a traced line at typical depth, within a foot or two is the working standard, and shallower or louder leaks tighten that further. The mark is always paired with a confidence read, and low-confidence results get confirmed by a second method before anyone cuts or digs.

Why would you come at night for detection?

Noise floor. Traffic, HVAC, appliances, and wind all compete with a leak's signal, and quiet hours can be the difference on a faint one. It is not routine, but for a stubborn, small, or deep leak, a late survey has closed cases the afternoon could not. Ask about it at (303) 552-3896.
